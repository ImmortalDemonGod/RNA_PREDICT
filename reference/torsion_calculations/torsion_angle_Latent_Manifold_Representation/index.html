    Latent Manifold

    : Heres an indepth proposal that explores whetherand howwe might devise a representation better than torsion angles for RNA modeling. Ill provide comprehensive mathematical rigor, detailed reasoning, and a threeway comparison (Cartesian, Torsion, and our Proposed Latent Manifold Representation).

    [emoji]: Below is the improved analysis.

    1. Introduction

    Current Representations: Cartesian Coordinates: Directly store every atoms (x, y, z). Total DoF: \approx 3 \times A \times N (with A being the number of atoms per residue). Advantages: Direct physical meaning, no assumptions about internal geometry. Disadvantages: High dimensionality; requires explicit constraints to enforce chemistry. Torsion Angles: Represent RNA via backbone dihedral angles (e.g., \alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta, \epsilon, \zeta, \chi plus sugar pucker parameters). Total DoF: \approx T \times N (typically T is 710). Advantages: Significantly fewer parameters and built-in enforcement of local geometry. Disadvantages: Still linear in N and may not easily capture long-range or global correlations (e.g., pseudoknots) without additional constraints.

    The Proposal: We propose a Learned Latent Manifold Representation (LLMR) that leverages data-driven dimensionality reduction. By analyzing a large ensemble of RNA structures, we can learn a mapping to a lower-dimensional latent space that captures both local and non-local (global) conformational constraints. This representation can potentially require far fewer degrees of freedom than even torsion angles, while naturally embedding physical and chemical constraints.

    1. The Proposed Latent Manifold Representation (LLMR)

    2.1. Concept and Rationale

    The core idea is to use machine learningvia techniques such as principal component analysis (PCA), autoencoders, or variational autoencoders (VAEs)to learn the intrinsic dimensionality of RNA conformations. Mathematical Formulation: 1. Data Collection: Let \theta \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N} represent the torsion angles of an RNA of N nucleotides. 2. Dimensionality Reduction: Find an encoder f: \mathbb{R}^{T \times N} \to \mathbb{R}^{d} with d \ll T \times N that maps the high-dimensional torsion space to a latent vector z. 3. Reconstruction: Use a decoder g: \mathbb{R}^{d} \to \mathbb{R}^{T \times N} to recover the torsion angles from the latent code. 4. Optimization: Train the system so that g(f(\theta)) \approx \theta for all training samples, while enforcing physical constraints (e.g., through loss terms that penalize deviations from known bond lengths/angles). Why This Can Be Better: Fewer Parameters: Instead of handling T \times N torsion angles, the latent space might require only d parameters where d may scale sublinearly or even be nearly constant for a given domain of RNA. Implicit Constraints: The latent manifold will naturally encode long-range correlations, complex topologies (e.g., pseudoknots), and domain-specific variations if trained on a representative dataset. Ease of Sampling & Optimization: A lower-dimensional latent space can vastly reduce the search space, making sampling, optimization, and even generative modeling more tractable.

    2.2. Mathematical Rigor Dimensionality Comparison: Suppose for an RNA of N nucleotides, the torsion angle representation has T \times N parameters. If statistical analysis reveals that the effective degrees of freedom are only d (with d \ll T \times N), then the reduction factor is: R = \frac{T \times N}{d} For example, if T = 8 and for N = 1000 we have 8000 parameters but our analysis finds that d = 200 (i.e., a 40 reduction), then: R = \frac{8000}{200} = 40 This suggests that the latent representation captures the essential conformational variability with far fewer parameters. Optimization Considerations: The loss function for training might combine reconstruction loss: \mathcal{L}{\text{recon}} = | \theta - g(f(\theta)) |^2, with additional regularization terms \mathcal{L}{\text{phys}} to enforce physical plausibility (e.g., deviations from ideal bond lengths/angles, steric clashes, etc.). The total loss is: \mathcal{L}{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}{\text{recon}} + \lambda \, \mathcal{L}_{\text{phys}}, where \lambda balances the reconstruction fidelity with physical realism.

    1. Discussion and Practical Implications Advantages of LLMR over Torsion Angles: Even Lower Dimensionality: By capturing the essential modes of RNA conformational variability, the latent space can reduce the number of effective degrees of freedom far beyond the constant-factor reduction offered by torsion angles. Implicit Global Constraints: LLMR can learn non-local correlations (e.g., between distant parts of the RNA, or complex topologies like pseudoknots) that are hard to capture with simple torsion angle parameterizations. Efficient Sampling: Optimization or generative sampling in a low-dimensional latent space is computationally cheaper and can lead to faster convergence. Challenges and Considerations: Training Data Dependency: The quality of the latent representation depends on having a sufficiently large and diverse dataset of RNA structures. Decoder Accuracy: The mapping g: \mathbb{R}^{d} \to \mathbb{R}^{T \times N} must accurately reconstruct physically valid torsion angles to ensure realistic 3D models. Generalizability: The learned manifold may be biased towards the conformations present in the training set; special care is needed to ensure it can generalize to novel or outlier RNA structures. Comparison Summary: Cartesian Coordinates: Pros: Direct, unambiguous, full 3D information. Cons: Very high-dimensional; needs heavy constraint enforcement. Torsion Angles: Pros: Lower-dimensional, inherently respects local chemistry. Cons: Still linear in N and may struggle with non-local correlations. Latent Manifold Representation: Pros: Potential for dramatic reduction in effective dimensionality, efficient sampling, and built-in global constraint encoding. Cons: Requires a robust training phase and careful validation to ensure accurate reconstruction and generalization.

    1. Conclusion

    Yes, there is a promising route to beat torsion angles for RNA modeling: by learning a latent manifold representation via dimensionality reduction techniques. This approach can compress the effective degrees of freedom to a level much lower than torsion angle spacewhile automatically capturing both local and global geometric constraints. Mathematically, if torsion space requires T \times N parameters and our learned latent space only requires d parameters with d \ll T \times N, then the optimization problem and the search space can be reduced by a factor of \frac{T \times N}{d}, which can be orders of magnitude smaller.

    In summary, while Cartesian and torsion representations offer linear scaling, a latent manifold approach holds the potential for even greater computational efficiency and improved physical plausibilityespecially for very large or complex RNA systems.

    : Its a common question: If we have a compressed representation (some latent code) that is more efficient than torsion angles, how can that be the actual output of an RNA structure predictor, since we ultimately want the 3D structure or angles? [emoji]: Heres how to think about it step by step.

    1. The Role of a Latent Representation

    When we talk about a learned, lower-dimensional or compressed representation for RNA conformation, its typically not an end in itselfrather, its an intermediate space that captures essential structural variability more compactly than torsion angles. In machine learning terms, think of an EncoderDecoder or Autoencoder setup: 1. Encoder \,f(\cdot): Takes a full structure (in some standard format3D coordinates, or torsion angles) and maps it to a low-dimensional vector z (the latent code). 2. Decoder \,g(\cdot): Takes the latent code z and expands it back to a full structure (e.g., torsions or 3D coordinates).

    This two-stage approach is typically used for training, analysis, or generative modeling. But once trained, we can invert the usage: Predict a latent code z directly from sequence (or other features) and then decode it into a final structure.

    1. Making the Latent Code the Output of Your Model

    2.1 Two-Part Model During Inference 1. Primary Model (Predictor): Input: RNA sequence (and possibly additional data like chemical probing, base-pair constraints, etc.). Output: The learned latent code z \in \mathbb{R}^{d}. 2. Decoder (Pretrained or Co-trained): Input: z. Output: Torsion angles or 3D coordinates (depending on how you set up the system).

    So from the outside users perspective, you might say:

    The final structure is produced by passing the predicted latent code through our decoder.

    Why have the model output the latent code first? Dimensionality & Efficiency: If the latent code is significantly smaller than the full set of torsion angles, it can simplify your main predictive network. You let the trained decoder handle all the details of converting that code into a physically valid structure. Modularization: You can easily update or refine the decoder without retraining the entire predictor, as long as the latent code format remains consistent. Interpretability (sometimes): Latent dimensions can capture high-level modes of conformation (e.g., global bending, helix orientation, etc.) that might be more interpretable than raw angles.

    2.2 Training Steps (High-Level) 1. Pretraining the Autoencoder (Optional): Gather a large set of known RNA structures (e.g., from PDB or simulation). Convert them into an existing representation (torsion angles or 3D). Train an encoderdecoder to compress them into a latent vector z and then reconstruct back to the original structure with minimal error. This yields a universal latent space that captures typical RNA geometry. 2. Training the Structure Predictor: Have a second neural network that, given an RNA sequence, predicts z. The reconstruction from z to angles/3D is done by the (frozen or co-trained) decoder. Minimize the final error in predicted structure (comparing the decoders output to the known ground-truth structure in your training set).

    In effect, the predictors final output is the compressed code z. Only after it passes through the decoder do you get the final, user-facing 3D coordinates or torsion angles.

    1. Why Not Just Predict 3D or Torsion Directly? Reduced search space: If your latent manifold is significantly smaller, your predictor can more easily learn from fewer parameters or more structured geometry. Implicit constraints: The decoder can encode complex RNA geometry (including ring pucker, base stacking, or even multi-strand constraints) so the predictor doesnt have to. Regularization: A well-learned latent space often disallows or penalizes unphysical conformations, so your predictor cant accidentally produce nonsense structures.

    In other words, if you trust your decoder to robustly turn z into valid structures, the predictor can focus on learning simpler latent coordinates, akin to how we often do factor-based representations in dimensionality reduction.

    1. Interpreting the Compressed Code

    4.1 Direct Use vs. Final Reconstitution Direct Use: Some pipelines might just keep z if all they need is an abstract representation (say, for classification or comparing similarity between structures). Final 3D: If your end-user or downstream application requires actual 3D coordinates (e.g., for molecular dynamics or visualization), you run the decoder to convert from z 3D.

    4.2 Human-Readable Meaning?

    A latent code can be partially interpretable if each dimension correlates with known structural modes. For instance, dimension 1 might roughly correspond to global bending angle of the helix, dimension 2 to degree of base-pair opening, and so forth. But that interpretability depends on how the system was trained and whether you impose any constraints or labeling on each dimension (e.g., a disentangled VAE approach).

    1. Summary Yes, you can absolutely have a compressed representation as the final output of an RNA structure predictor. The uservisible 3D structure is obtained by plugging that lowdimensional code into a decoder (co-trained or pretrained) that handles the job of reconstructing physically valid coordinates or angles. This approach effectively means you have a two-stage inference pipeline:
      1. Predict latent code from sequence.
      2. Decode latent code into 3D (or torsion angles).

    Bottom line: Outputting a compressed representation is perfectly viable if you also have a reliable process to expand that representation back to the standard format (3D or torsion). This two-step architecture can yield significant advantages in training efficiency, representation power, and capturing real-world RNA geometry within a smaller parameter set.

    [emoji]: Below is a stepbystep outline of your options and whats next, depending on what exactly you want your autoencoder (or other neural model) to learn and output.

    1. Clarify Your End Goal
      1. Autoencoder for Torsion Angles \leftrightarrow 3D Goal: Learn an invertible mapping between torsionangle space and 3D structure. Typical Setup:
      2. Encoder: 3D \to Torsion angles (or Torsion angles \to Latent code).
      3. Decoder: Torsion angles \to 3D (or Latent code \to Torsion angles \to 3D). Data Needed: Pairs of (\text{torsion angles}), (\text{3D coords}). You can generate torsion angles by backcalculating them from your known 3D structures.
      4. Autoencoder for Latent Representation \leftrightarrow Torsion Angles Goal: If you want an even more compressed representation than plain torsion angles, you might do an autoencoder that takes torsion angles in, outputs torsion angles, but uses an internal compressed latent code. Data Needed: Torsion angles for a large set of RNA structures. (Youd still typically calculate them from 3D coordinates initiallybut the main training is in torsion space.)
      5. Direct 3D \leftrightarrow 3D Autoencoder Goal: Compress 3D structures into a latent code, then reconstruct the same 3D structure. (Skipping torsion angles altogether in the direct input/output, but you might still incorporate them as a regularization or constraint.) Data Needed: A large set of 3D RNA structures.

    So the immediate step depends on which representation you want your autoencoder to take in and which representation you want it to spit out.

    1. If Your Autoencoder Inputs Torsion Angles and Outputs 3D
      1. Gather/Generate Torsion Angles: Take your existing library of RNA 3D structures (PDB, cryo-EM, etc.). Backcalculate the torsion angles for each residue. This step is straightforward vector math (the standard dihedral formula). Now you have (\text{angle vector}, \text{3D coords}) pairs.
      2. Design the Autoencoder or Model: Encoder: Goes from torsion angles to a latent code. Decoder: Takes that latent code and predicts either:
      3. Torsion angles again (for a pure autoencoder), and then a separate kinematics step to get 3D.
      4. 3D coordinates directly. In many pipelines, its actually a Decoder + Kinematics approach: The decoder outputs refined torsion angles, and a fixed forward-kinematics routine maps angles \to 3D.
      5. Training: Youll minimize a reconstruction loss in 3D space (e.g., RMSD) plus any geometry/physics terms you want (to avoid nonphysical bond lengths, etc.). Optionally, you can also include a direct torsion-angle reconstruction loss if your decoder explicitly outputs angles.
      6. Inference: Input: Torsion angles (perhaps from partial predictions or from an RNA sequencebased predictor). Output: 3D coords, via \text{Decoder}(z) \rightarrow \text{Torsion}\rightarrow \text{3D}.

    Answer to your question: Do we now need torsion angles so we can train the autoencoder to learn the mapping to 3D? Yes, if your pipelines input to the autoencoder is angles and the output is 3D (or angles that get converted to 3D), you do need to precompute those angles from your reference dataset.

    1. Potential Workflows

    Workflow A: Direct Torsion \leftrightarrow 3D Autoencoder 1. Dataset Prep: For each RNA structure, store \bigl(\mathbf{\theta}, \mathbf{X}\bigr), where \mathbf{\theta} = torsion angles, \mathbf{X} = 3D coordinates. 2. Model: Encoder: \mathbf{\theta} \to z. Decoder: z \to \mathbf{\theta}^. Then a known forward-kinematics function K(\cdot) maps \mathbf{\theta}^ \to \mathbf{X}^. Loss: |\mathbf{X}^ - \mathbf{X}| plus possible torsion/physics terms.

    Workflow B: Torsion \leftrightarrow Torsion Autoencoder, Then Torsion \to 3D 1. Model: Train a standard autoencoder in torsion space only (i.e., no direct 3D reconstruction). 2. Kinematics: After the autoencoders decoder yields \mathbf{\theta}^, you have a separate function K that does \mathbf{\theta}^ \to \mathbf{X}^. 3. Loss: Might be purely |\mathbf{\theta} - \mathbf{\theta}^|. However, you might add a 3D alignment loss in parallel if you want the angles to reflect physical correctness.

    Workflow C: 3D \leftrightarrow 3D Autoencoder (Skipping Torsions) You can do a direct 3D3D autoencoder, but then you typically rely on a physically informed loss or constraints to ensure bond lengths, angles, ring closures, etc., remain realistic.

    1. Why Bother With Torsion Angles for the Autoencoder? Lower Dimensional: Torsion angles drastically cut down the parameter space compared to raw 3D coordinates. Implicit Local Constraints: Angles automatically fix bond lengths and angles, meaning you wont have to teach your model that those are inviolable. Aligns With Existing Tools: Many RNA modeling pipelines operate in angle space (Rosetta, for instance).

    Hence, its very common to do:

    Autoencoder: Angles \to latent code \to angles \to 3D.

    1. Putting It All Together Yes, if your plan is to have an autoencoder that learns a mapping from angles to 3D (or angles \to angles \to 3D), youll need a set of torsion angles for training. The straightforward next step is:
      1. Take your reference 3D structures (the same ones youre presumably using to verify embeddings).
      2. Compute torsion angles for each residue.
      3. Pair those angles with the original 3D coordinates in your training set.
      4. Train your chosen autoencoder approach to minimize reconstruction error.

    In other words, you do want the torsion angles if your autoencoders fundamental input (or output) is RNA torsion angles, because thats how it will learn the correct mapping to physically valid 3D conformations.

    Final Advice / Next Step 1. Implement Torsion Computation: Write (or grab a library) to parse each residues \alpha, \beta, \gamma, \dots, \chi angles from your 3D references. 2. Assemble the Dataset: (\mathbf{\theta}_i, \mathbf{X}i) pairs for i=1 to N{\text{structures}}. 3. Define the Autoencoder: Decide if you want to decode directly to 3D or decode back to angles. 4. Train: Minimizing a 3D RMSD or angle-level reconstruction error (plus any physical constraints).

    If your local attention and embeddings are working, you now incorporate this angle3D stage as the geometry module or autoencoder module.
